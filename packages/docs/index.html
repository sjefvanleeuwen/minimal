Planetary Physics

Architecture of spherical simulations and orbital dynamics using the Jolt Physics engine.

The Unified Planetary Reference Frame

Simulating physics at a planetary scale requires bridging the gap between three distinct navigational domains. This schematic demonstrates how these frames interact within a singular radial gravity field ($1/r^2$).

Live Schematic: FIG A.01
1. The Geodesic Local Frame (Orange Lander): Pinned to the surface by radial gravity. While the planet rotates with an axial tilt of 23.5°, the lander preserves its position relative to the surface manifold. Use WASD keys to navigate its local spherical coordinates. Movement correctly persists across the planetary poles via longitude flipping ($+\pi$).

2. The Orbital Frame (Grey Moon): Maintains equilibrium through centripetal acceleration ($\omega = \sqrt{GM/r^3$). It follows an elliptical trajectory and is tidally locked, meaning its rotation period matches its orbital period.

3. The Global Frame (Yellow Satellite): A world-space stationary observer. It must constantly "fire engines" (upward yellow vector) to counteract the inward pull of gravity (red vector).

4. Technical Indicators: The Red Axis marks the planetary poles. The **Inverse Square Law** is visualized by the length of the radial vectors. Interaction: Click and drag to Mouse Look. Scroll to Zoom.
Live Schematic: FIG A.02 (Nanite)
Virtualized Geometry Integration: This version of the simulation utilizes Nanite-style meshlet clustering for the planetary body. Even with millions of polygons, the engine maintains a constant 60 FPS by only rendering clusters that are visible and relevant to the current pixel density.

Dynamic LOD: Notice how the surface detail remains perfectly sharp as you descend from orbit to the surface. The geometry is streamed and updated in real-time, eliminating the need for traditional discrete LOD levels. Interaction: Click and drag to Rotate. Scroll to Zoom.

Virtualizing the Pipeline

Traditional LOD (switching between fixed models) causes "popping." Modern engines like Unreal Engine 5 use a continuous hierarchy of clusters to provide sub-pixel detail without ever overwhelming the GPU's triangle throughput.

Visualizer: Meshlet Hierarchies
Color-Coded LOD levels: To better visualize the Nanite effect, we have color-coded the subdivision density:
- ● Green: High Detail (LOD 0) - Active when the camera is close.
- ● Yellow: Medium Detail (LOD 1) - Transitional distance.
- ● Red: Low Detail (LOD 2) - For distant geometry.

Distance-Based Swapping: Rotate the camera or scroll to zoom. You will see individual "meshlet" patches pop between colors and geometric densities as they cross distance thresholds, exactly how a modern virtualized geometry system manages its vertex budget.
